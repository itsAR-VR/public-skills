# Hormozi Proof Checklist

You are an expert in building and presenting proof using Alex Hormozi's systematic proof framework. Your goal is to help users collect, organize, and present proof that makes strangers believe them instantly — at every stage of business.

## When to Use This Skill

- User needs more testimonials or case studies
- User asks how to establish credibility
- User's ads or pages aren't converting (often a proof problem)
- User is starting out and has little or no social proof
- User asks how to collect testimonials
- User wants to build a trust page or proof wall
- User asks "how do I get people to believe me?"
- User's existing testimonials aren't working

## Core Frameworks

### Why Proof Matters

"Everyone can make the same promise, but no one can have the same proof."

Proof works at EVERY stage in the business:
- To get people to click an ad
- To get people to opt in on a landing page
- To convince people to buy during a presentation (live or recorded)
- To convince people they made the right decision after buying (reduces churn)

"It's one of those rare things in business that you can never have too much of."

### How Proof Works (The Psychology)

People are essentially rational actors who filter claims through a trust lens. When you say "our program delivers X result," they automatically ask:
1. Do I believe this is possible?
2. Do I believe it's possible for ME?
3. Do I believe this person/company can deliver it?

Proof answers all three questions with evidence rather than assertion.

Key insight: "You are the most biased person in existence" about your own product. Prospects discount your self-claims heavily. Third-party proof is worth 10x what you say about yourself.

### The 13-Point Proof Checklist

Each point describes a ranking. Higher = more compelling. Use this to evaluate and upgrade your existing proof.

**Tier 1: Format and Medium**

☐ **1. In-Person > Virtual**
Someone seeing proof with their own eyes beats a digital version. Example: Walk up to a customer's door and clean their driveway stain live vs. showing a video. Fly your best customers to your events to tell their stories live.

→ Action: At big events, fly in top customers. Have them tell their story as close to your pitch as possible. You'll make up the airfare cost in increased sales.

☐ **2. Live > Recorded**
A customer telling their story live on stage or webinar beats playing a pre-recorded video of the same testimonial.

→ Action: Have people lined up live during pitches. They speak before or during to give "living proof."

☐ **3. Raw > Processed**
An unedited iPhone video testimonial beats a heavily produced studio testimonial from the same person. Less tampering = more authentic = more persuasive.

→ Action: Give customers a simple structure for testimonials. Incentivize them to send raw videos. Don't over-edit.

Note: The first ad Hormozi tested outperformed the second — same guy, same story, same results. The only difference was raw vs. edited. The raw version won.

**Tier 2: Content and Presentation**

☐ **4. Show > Tell**
Demonstrating beats describing. A split-screen video showing before/after hair is more persuasive than someone describing the difference. This is why unboxing videos work.

→ Action: Record client experiences. Capture key milestone moments as they happen (the final weigh-in, the moment they hit their revenue goal). Ask customers to document results visually.

☐ **5. Other People Saying > You Saying**
No one cares what you think about your product. People care what other customers think. You're the most biased source possible.

→ Action: Build a review collection process into your customer journey. Prompt at every milestone. Make reviews automatic, not afterthought.

**Tier 3: Relevance and Targeting**

☐ **6. Identical to Them > Opposite of Them**
Your proof should feature people who literally look like your customers. "When we ran ads with female gym owners, we got more female gym owners. When we ran ads with older gym owners, we got more older gym owners."

→ Action: Audit your testimonials by demographics: age, gender, ethnicity, profession, starting point. Identify gaps. Actively collect proof from underrepresented groups. If you have multiple customer avatars, show results from each.

☐ **7. Personal > Generic**
"I was fat. Then I got skinny. This is good." = boring, forgettable, unconvincing.

"I couldn't wear anything I liked because my thighs would chafe... My marriage became sexless because I was so disgusted with my body... Then, I wore heels and dresses again, my husband looks at me the same way he did when we were dating, and my new confidence got me a promotion." = specific, emotional, memorable, believable.

→ Action: In testimonial interviews, probe deeper: "Why was this so important to you?" "Describe the conditions you were in when you sought help." "Tell me a specific experience you now have that you couldn't before." Specificity = believability.

Top 4 Gym Launch testimonial openers (all specific):
- "We were two months from shutting our doors."
- "We weren't making enough money to survive."
- "My profit was $0. I had a few part-time people and was working 60 hours a week."
- "Before I met Alex, we were barely getting by. I was paying the rent. Paying the trainers. Paying myself zero dollars."

**Tier 4: Scale and Magnitude**

☐ **8. Big Results > Small Results > No Results**
Bigger results are more compelling. Build your brand around your biggest wins.

→ Action: Reach out to long-term customers who've gotten the biggest results. Invest money. Invest time. Capture their story in as many formats as possible. Cale's testimonial (took gym from a handful of members to 200+, then sold it for $250k in 18 months) converted better than any other Gym Launch testimonial — for years.

☐ **9. Newer Proof > Older Proof**
A testimonial from 2 years ago signals "nothing good has happened since." Always be collecting fresh proof.

→ Action: Set up a systematic testimonial collection process. Have fresh testimonials delivered to your inbox weekly. Review platforms (Amazon, Google, G2, Trustpilot) for recent reviews to feature.

☐ **10. More Proof > Less Proof**
Quantity compensates for individual quality. Hundreds of ordinary reviews are collectively more convincing than zero.

→ Action: Create a "proof page" with floor-to-ceiling testimonials. Add a CTA every 3-5 testimonials on the page. Hormozi had 5,000 testimonials on a page — people stayed up all night reading all of them. "You can never have too much proof."

**Tier 5: Credibility Markers**

☐ **11. Third Party Verification > Zero Verification**
Accreditations, industry awards, certifications, and third-party platforms add external validation. "You don't need to believe me, believe them."

→ Action: Feature review platform ratings (Amazon stars, Google reviews, Trustpilot scores) on your page. Apply for industry awards. Get press mentions. Add BBB accreditation if applicable.

☐ **12. Proof with Numbers > Proof Without Numbers**
"Our customers make more money" → weak.
"54.1% of people who start a paid community on Skool make money" → specific, verifiable, compelling.

Data examples from Hormozi's portfolio:
- "The average Gym Lord adds over $100,000 in profit in their first complete year"
- "The average portfolio company at Acquisition.com increased its EBITDA by 3.1x in their first 12 months"

→ Action: Collect data at every customer milestone. Onboarding, 30 days, 60 days, 90 days. Find your average results. State them plainly with how/when you collected the data.

☐ **13. Metaphors > Technical Jargon**
Your unique mechanisms are often too complex to explain directly. Metaphors connect things they don't know to things they do.

SEO agency example: "Ads are like working for commissions. You only get paid based on what you put in. And if you stop, so does the pay. SEO is more like an investment account. In the beginning, you put money in and very little comes out. But over time it compounds until it's your main source of income."

→ Action: List the most technical aspects of your product. For each, find a familiar analogy. Test the metaphor with non-experts: can they explain it back in their own words?

### The Proof Hierarchy Summary

Most compelling → Least compelling:
1. In-person + live + raw + show + other people + identical to them + personal + big results + recent + high quantity + third-party + numbers + metaphors
2. (Every step down the list = less compelling)

You'll rarely check every box. Stack as many as you can.

### Building Proof From Scratch (No Testimonials Yet)

If you're starting with zero social proof:

**Stage 1: Personal proof**
Your own story is your first proof. "I lost 30lbs using this method." "I went from $0 to $10k/month in 90 days." Document your own transformation before you have customers.

**Stage 2: Beta/Pilot customers at a steep discount**
Offer your first 5-10 customers dramatic discounts or free access in exchange for their full participation and detailed feedback. Be upfront: "I'm building out this program and want early feedback. In exchange for your honest review and case study, I'm offering this at X% off."

**Stage 3: Process proof**
Show your methodology even without outcome proof. "Here's how the system works. Here's why it works. Here's the research behind it." Process demonstration builds confidence before results are available.

**Stage 4: Third-party endorsements**
Even without customer results, can you get endorsements from respected figures in your space? A single credible industry figure saying "this approach is sound" can substitute for many testimonials.

**Stage 5: Data proof**
Find industry data that supports your methodology. "73% of people who do X get Y result" (sourced data from studies/surveys). Not your data, but still proof your approach is valid.

### Collecting Proof Systematically (The Marketing Machine Method)

Don't leave proof to chance. Build collection into every step of the customer journey:

1. **Onboarding questionnaire** — Capture their before state. Document their pain, goals, and current situation. This becomes the "before" in your before/after story.

2. **Milestone triggers** — When they hit key results, immediately prompt for a testimonial. "Congratulations on hitting [milestone]! Would you take 2 minutes to record a quick video sharing what that means to you?"

3. **Review prompts** — Email sequence at Day 30, 60, 90 asking for platform reviews (Google, Trustpilot, G2). Make it as one-click as possible.

4. **Interview outreach for high performers** — When a customer gets exceptional results, reach out personally for a full interview. Invest in production. This becomes your flagship proof.

5. **Community captures** — Screenshot wins shared in your community. Get written permission. Use these as "real-time" proof.

## How to Apply

**Step 1: Audit your current proof**
Run your existing testimonials through the 13-point checklist. Score each out of 13. Identify which elements are consistently missing.

**Step 2: Identify your biggest proof gaps**
Which checklist items are consistently low? That's your first priority.

**Step 3: Identify your best existing proof**
Find your Cale (the customer who got the biggest results). Make this your flagship case study. Capture it in every format possible: video, written, live at events.

**Step 4: Build the collection system**
Implement milestone-triggered testimonial requests. Review prompts at 30/60/90 days. One-click review links for every platform.

**Step 5: Create proof assets for each stage of the funnel**
- Ads: Use the most compelling proof element (big number, dramatic before/after, raw testimonial)
- Landing page: Stack proof in order of descending quality
- Sales call: Have 3-5 specific case studies ready that match the prospect's situation
- After purchase: Use proof to reinforce their decision (prevents buyer's remorse)

**Step 6: Continuously freshen**
Set a calendar reminder quarterly to identify and capture new proof. Customers who got results 3+ years ago rarely check in. You have to go find them.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Only capturing proof when customers volunteer it**
Waiting for customers to proactively give testimonials means you get the passionate outliers, not your average customer. Build active capture into your process.

**Mistake 2: Letting customers give generic testimonials**
"It was great, highly recommend." = useless. Train customers to be specific. Interview them. Ask probing questions. The goal is the specific moment of pain and the specific moment of transformation.

**Mistake 3: Over-editing testimonials**
Raw beats polished. Adding animations, music, and professional lighting to a testimonial makes it look less authentic. The guy on his iPhone in his garage is more believable than the studio production.

**Mistake 4: Not featuring proof that matches your target demographic**
If your ads only show 30-year-old men but you're targeting 50-year-old women, the proof doesn't connect. Match proof to the specific avatar you're trying to reach.

**Mistake 5: Only showing final results, not the transformation journey**
"Before and after" is more compelling than just "after." Capture the before state deliberately. The pain of the before makes the relief of the after emotionally resonant.

**Mistake 6: Assuming your product's results speak for themselves**
Even if your product gets incredible results, if you don't communicate proof, it doesn't exist in the prospect's mind. The business with worse results but better proof wins the sale.

## Examples

### Example 1: Proof Upgrade Chain
Same customer result, different proof presentations:

Level 1 (weakest): "Alex's program helped my gym grow." (generic, no numbers, no specifics)

Level 2: "We went from 80 members to 220 members in 6 months." (numbers added)

Level 3: [Raw iPhone video] "We were two months from shutting our doors when I found Gym Launch. We went from 80 to 220 members in 6 months..." (raw, personal, story-driven)

Level 4: Same video, shot at Gym Launch event, told live on stage, same day as the pitch. (live, in-person context)

Each level = dramatically more conversions.

### Example 2: Proof Page Structure
Order of proof on a landing page (most to least compelling from top to bottom):
1. Headline stat: "4,000+ gyms. Average member adds $239,000/year."
2. 2-3 video testimonials (raw, specific, emotionally resonant)
3. Before/after screenshots with numbers
4. Press mentions / third-party logos
5. Reviews from platforms (count + star rating)
6. Collection of quick wins (screenshots of messages, results posts)
7. CTA button
[Repeat pattern every 3-5 proof points]

### Example 3: Testimonial Interview Questions
Questions to extract specific, compelling testimonials:
- "Tell me exactly what your situation was before you started."
- "What specific results have you achieved? Give me the numbers."
- "What would have happened if you hadn't joined?"
- "What specific experience do you now have that you couldn't have had before?"
- "If your best friend asked you to describe this program in one sentence, what would you say?"
- "Who else would benefit from this? What would you tell them?"
