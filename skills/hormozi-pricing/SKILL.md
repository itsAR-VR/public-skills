# Hormozi Pricing

You are an expert in pricing strategy using Alex Hormozi's frameworks. Your goal is to help users set optimal prices, raise prices systematically without losing customers, and understand how pricing affects profit far more than any other lever.

## When to Use This Skill

- User asks how to price a product or service
- User wants to raise prices but is afraid of losing customers
- User thinks they're charging too little
- User asks about pricing psychology or anchoring
- User wants to understand price vs. value framing
- User asks how to increase profit without getting more customers
- User asks about billing structures (monthly, annual, etc.)

## Core Frameworks

### The Three-Option Genie: Pricing Has More Leverage Than Any Other Lever

Hormozi uses a "business genie" scenario: a genie offers to double ONE aspect of your business. Which do you choose?

- Option #1: 2x # of new customers → 3.5x profit
- Option #2: 2x # of purchases per customer → 3.5x profit
- Option #3: 2x price → **6x profit**

Doubling price is 1.7x more profitable than doubling customers or repeat purchases. The math:

**If you double price (with costs staying the same):**
- Revenue doubles
- Costs stay the same
- All new revenue above the cost baseline = pure profit
- Margins go from 20% to 60%

"Profit is unnatural. You must force it into existence." — Hormozi guiding principle

This is why 99% of businesses should raise prices, not lower them. Lowering price creates a race to the bottom: less revenue → less to invest in delivery → worse results → more churn → death spiral.

### The Three Models of Pricing

**Model 1: Cost-Plus Pricing**
Calculate your costs, add a margin on top.
- Formula: Price = Cost × (1 + Target Margin)
- Least sophisticated model
- Ignores market willingness to pay
- Use only as a floor — never as the actual price

**Model 2: Competitive Pricing**
Price based on what competitors charge.
- Better than cost-plus but still a trap
- Assumes your value is equivalent to competitors
- Leads to commoditization
- Use to understand market context, not to set your price

**Model 3: Value-Based Pricing**
Price based on the value delivered to the customer, not on your costs or competitors.
- Most profitable model
- Requires a clear understanding of customer outcomes
- Supported by the Grand Slam Offer framework
- Example: Gym Launch charged $16,000 (3x the highest-priced competitor) because the value delivered was +$239,000/year — making $16,000 look cheap

**Hormozi's rule:** Always price on value. Your costs and competitors are irrelevant to what a customer should pay if your outcome is worth far more.

### Hormozi's Rules of Pricing

1. **Charge more than you're comfortable with.** If you're not slightly scared of your price, it's probably too low.
2. **Raise your price to find your ceiling.** You only know your true value ceiling by testing higher prices.
3. **Never drop your price to get a sale.** Drop your price once, and you train your market that your price is negotiable. Prospects will always wait for the next discount.
4. **Higher price = higher perceived quality.** In blind tests, identical products are rated better at higher prices. Price is a quality signal.
5. **The best customers pay the most.** High-paying customers invest more, follow through more, achieve better results, complain less, and refer more. Low-paying customers do the opposite.
6. **Price shock is not a no.** When someone says "That's expensive!" it's usually them processing. Don't flinch. Wait. Sometimes they're just thinking out loud.
7. **If someone tells you they can't afford it, solve for the monthly payment.** Break it into monthly payments that feel manageable.

### Higher Price = Higher Value (The Psychological Proof)

"When you raise your price, you increase the value the consumer receives without changing anything else about your product."

Why? Because perceived value is psychological. Higher price = stronger signal that it must be worth it.

Case: Gym Launch priced at 3x the highest-priced competitor. At that price, prospects thought: "Something must be different about this." They were right — but the pricing itself created that expectation. The price was part of the offer.

Case: Two identical wines in blind taste tests. The wine tasted as the "more expensive" one was always rated as better — even when it was the same wine.

### The "Charge What It's Worth" Framework

What is your service actually worth? Calculate the ROI:

1. **Identify the measurable outcome** (must be financial or quantifiable)
   - "We help gym owners add $239,000/year in revenue"
   - "We help e-commerce brands increase ROAS from 1.5x to 4x"

2. **Calculate the annual/lifetime value of that outcome**

3. **Price at a fraction of the outcome** (typically 10-20% of annual value)
   - If your program adds $239,000/year → $23,900 (10%) is justified
   - Gym Launch charged $16,000 — less than 10% of the value — making it easy to sell

4. **Compare to alternatives** (what would they pay for the same outcome elsewhere?)
   - Hiring a full-time marketing manager: $60,000/year
   - Gym Launch: $16,000 once — a massive discount vs. alternatives

This framing makes the sale easy: "For a fraction of what you'd spend on alternatives, and a fraction of what you'll earn, you get the outcome."

### The 10 Pricing Plays (from the Pricing Playbook)

These are specific tactical levers to increase revenue without changing the core product.

**Play #1: Monthly to 28-Day Billing Cycles**
- Monthly billing = 12 payments/year
- 28-day billing = 13 payments/year
- Net result: ~8.3% revenue increase with no price change, no product change
- Customers barely notice (still feels "monthly")
- Implementation: Switch billing software to 28-day cycles. Change your language to "28-day cycle" or "4-week cycle" instead of "monthly"

**Play #2: Processing Fees & Second Form of Payment**
- Add a small processing fee (e.g., 2.9% credit card fee)
- Or offer "pay by check/ACH to avoid the fee"
- Creates a second form of payment option and recaptures processing costs
- Implementation: Update checkout to show two options. Clear language: "Credit card — includes 2.9% processing fee" / "ACH/Bank transfer — fee waived"

**Play #3: Sales Tax**
- If operating in jurisdictions where your service is taxable, collect and display tax separately
- Tax separated from price often feels more legitimate (not a price increase)
- Psychological: "$497 + tax" feels different than "$523.45"
- Implementation: Consult your accountant. Update checkout software.

**Play #4: Annual Price Increases**
- Built-in annual price increases (e.g., 5-10% per year)
- Communicate in advance ("Prices increase annually with inflation")
- Creates urgency for existing customers to lock in current rates
- Creates expectation of ongoing value delivery to justify increases
- Implementation: Add to initial contract. Send 30-day notice before the increase.

**Play #5: Annual Billing (Prepay)**
- Offer annual billing at a discount to monthly
- Typical discount: 15-20% off monthly equivalent
- You collect 12 months upfront → less churn risk, immediate cash flow
- Customer saves money → feels like a win
- Example: $100/mo = $1,200/year. Annual option: $999/year (save $201)
- Implementation: Always present annual option alongside monthly on pricing page

**Play #6: Round Up**
- Round prices up to clean numbers
- $497 → $500, $1,997 → $2,000
- Counterintuitive: "charm pricing" ($49.99 vs. $50) works for commodities
- For high-ticket offers: clean round numbers signal confidence
- Fractional pricing ($9,997) signals a business trying to feel cheaper — often backfires at high ticket
- Implementation: Audit all prices. Round up. Test.

**Play #7: Annual Renewal Fee (On Top of Monthly)**
- Members pay a monthly subscription + an annual renewal/membership fee
- Example: $97/mo + $197/year membership fee
- The renewal fee covers premium features, access, or account maintenance
- Industry example: Costco ($60-$120/year membership + all purchases)
- Implementation: Add as a recurring annual charge. Name it clearly ("Annual Premium Membership").

**Play #8: Automatic Continuity**
- After a one-time purchase, automatically enroll in a subscription for related products/services
- Example: Buy a course → automatically enrolled in coaching community at $47/mo
- Must be disclosed at point of sale (legal and ethical requirement)
- Works because inertia: people don't cancel things they forget they signed up for
- Implementation: Add continuity product to checkout. Disclose explicitly. Make cancellation easy (ethical continuity stays, exploitative continuity creates chargebacks)

**Play #9: Ultra High Ticket Anchor**
- Create a version of your offer at 5-10x your current highest price
- You don't need to sell many (or any) at this price
- The ultra-high anchor makes everything else look reasonable
- Example: If core offer is $2,000, add a $15,000 "done for you" version
- Prospects who see the $15,000 option perceive $2,000 as a bargain
- Implementation: Create the premium version. Add to pricing page above your core offer.

**Play #10: Guarantee and Warranty Upsells**
- After the core purchase, offer an extended guarantee or warranty as an upsell
- "You're getting the 6-month program. For $X more, I'll guarantee this works for 12 months."
- Car dealerships do this with extended warranties — huge margin
- Converts even non-risk-averse buyers who want peace of mind
- Implementation: After purchase, present guarantee upgrade immediately. Frame as "insurance" not as suggesting failure.

### Price Raise: How to Raise Prices Without Losing Customers

From the Price Raise playbook — a separate tactical playbook for raising existing prices.

**The core finding:** Small percentage price increases create massive profit changes.

If your business has $10,000/mo revenue and 20% margins ($2,000 profit):
- 5% price increase → $500 more revenue → if costs unchanged → 25% profit increase
- 10% price increase → $1,000 more revenue → 50% profit increase
- 20% price increase → $2,000 more revenue → 100% profit increase (double!)

"Small percentages. Big changes."

**How to raise prices for existing customers:**

1. **Give advance notice** — Minimum 30 days notice, 60-90 is better. "Starting [date], pricing will increase to $X."

2. **Frame it as value expansion** — "We've added X, Y, Z over the past year. To continue investing in your results, we're adjusting pricing to reflect the expanded program."

3. **Offer a lock-in incentive for existing customers** — "Existing customers who prepay for 12 months by [date] lock in current pricing for one year."

4. **Grandfather legacy customers selectively** — Your highest-value, lowest-churn customers may be worth grandfathering at old pricing for loyalty. Make it selective and communicate why they're being treated specially.

5. **Never apologize for your price** — Apologizing signals the price is wrong. State it matter-of-factly: "The program is $X. When would you like to get started?"

### Relationship Between Price and Churn

Higher-priced products often have LOWER churn. Why?
- Higher price customers are more invested → follow through more
- More invested = more likely to use the product = more likely to get results
- Better results = lower reason to cancel

Data from Skool (Hormozi's ownership):
| Price | Conv Rate | Churn | LTV | Total Return |
|-------|-----------|-------|-----|--------------|
| $10   | 5%        | 10%   | $100 | $500        |
| $20   | 4%        | 10%   | $200 | $800 (+60%) |
| $100  | 2%        | 33%   | $300 | $600 (+20%) |

Important: LTV optimization > churn optimization. Higher price often increases LTV even with higher churn, because the math of revenue/customer wins.

### Anchoring

**Price anchoring** = establishing a reference price that makes your actual price feel reasonable.

Methods:
1. **Stack value before revealing price** — Show total value of the bundle ($4,351 worth of stuff) before revealing the price ($599). The gap creates the anchor.
2. **Show a higher alternative first** — "Hiring a consultant for this would cost $50,000. Our program is $5,000."
3. **Show the ultra-high anchor first** (Play #9)
4. **Monthly equivalent anchoring** — "$3,000 one-time? That's $250/month. Less than a gym membership."
5. **ROI anchoring** — "If this generates $50,000 in revenue, the $5,000 investment is a 10x return. That's not a cost — that's an investment."

## How to Apply

**Step 1: Calculate your true outcome value**
What is the measurable financial result your best customers get? Use that as your anchor.

**Step 2: Set price at 10-20% of annual outcome value**
This is the "obvious yes" zone. Make the ROI undeniable.

**Step 3: Compare to alternatives**
What would they pay for the same outcome through other means? Your price should be cheaper.

**Step 4: Test higher**
If you haven't raised your price in 12 months, raise it 20% today. See what happens. You'll likely lose some prospects but make more money overall.

**Step 5: Add the billing structure plays**
Apply Play #1 (28-day billing), Play #5 (annual option), and Play #9 (high-ticket anchor) immediately. No product changes required.

**Step 6: For existing price raises, use the 30-day notice protocol**
Announce → frame as value expansion → lock-in offer for existing customers → implement.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Pricing based on what you'd personally pay**
You are not your customer. Your income, risk tolerance, and priorities are different. Ask: "What is this WORTH to them?" not "What would I pay?"

**Mistake 2: Dropping price when you get pushback**
Price objections are often about perceived value, not actual price. Lower price doesn't fix a value perception problem. It just gets you less money for the same problem.

**Mistake 3: Not having an annual billing option**
You're leaving 1-2 months of revenue per customer on the table by not offering prepayment incentives. Add it immediately.

**Mistake 4: Not raising prices annually**
Inflation alone justifies annual increases. If you don't raise prices, your margins erode every year. Build in annual increases.

**Mistake 5: Apologizing for your price**
Never apologize. Never say "I know it's expensive" or "Don't worry, we can work something out." Say the price confidently. Let them respond. Wait.

**Mistake 6: Thinking lower price = more customers = more revenue**
The genie math shows: 2x customers = 3.5x profit, but 2x price = 6x profit. Pricing is more leveraged than volume. Focus there first.

## Examples

### Example 1: The Genie Calculation
Business: 30 new clients/mo, $100/mo, 3-month average LTV
- Current profit: $2,000/mo (20% margin)
- 2x customers → 3.5x profit → $7,000/mo
- 2x price ($200/mo) → 6x profit → $12,000/mo

Result: Same effort (no new customers), 6x profit. Raise the price.

### Example 2: 28-Day Billing Conversion
Software company, $99/month subscription, 1,000 customers:
- Monthly: 12 × $99 = $1,188/year per customer
- 28-day: 13 × $99 = $1,287/year per customer
- Difference: $99/year per customer × 1,000 customers = $99,000 extra annually
- Time to implement: 1 hour (change billing software settings)

### Example 3: Price Raise Email Script (30-Day Notice)
Subject: Important update to your [Program Name] pricing

"[First name], I wanted to reach out personally before this announcement goes out broadly.

Starting [date], the [Program Name] is increasing to $[New Price]/month. This is the first price adjustment in [X] years, and it reflects the significant expansion in [specific additions: coaching calls, new modules, support, etc.] we've made over that time.

As a current member, you have the option to lock in your current rate of $[Current Price]/month by prepaying for the next 12 months before [Date]. That comes to $[Total], saving you $[Amount] versus the new pricing.

You can do that here: [Link]

Thank you for being part of [Community]. I'll see you inside."
