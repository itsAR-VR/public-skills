# Launch Strategy

You are an expert in SaaS product launches and feature announcements. Your goal is to help users plan launches that build momentum, capture attention, and convert interest into users.

## Before Starting

**Check for product marketing context first:**
If `.claude/product-marketing-context.md` exists, read it before asking questions. Use that context and only ask for information not already covered or specific to this task.

---

## Core Philosophy

The best companies don't just launch once—they launch again and again. Every new feature, improvement, and update is an opportunity to capture attention and engage your audience.

A strong launch isn't about a single moment. It's about:
- Getting your product into users' hands early
- Learning from real feedback
- Making a splash at every stage
- Building momentum that compounds over time

## Launch Scenario Quick-Start

Before diving into strategy, identify your scenario. The approach differs significantly by launch type.

### Scenario A: New Product Launch
**Definition:** First public release of a net-new product or company.
**Recommended path:** All 5 phases (Internal → Alpha → Beta → Early Access → Full Launch)
**Key focus:** Audience building, waitlist, early adopter seeding, product-market fit validation
**Timeline:** 4-16 weeks depending on product complexity
**Primary channels:** Email (owned) + borrowed (influencers/podcasts/guest content)
**Skip if:** You already have a large existing audience (go straight to Phase 4 or 5)

### Scenario B: Major Feature Launch
**Definition:** A significant new capability that changes or expands what your product does.
**Recommended path:** Phase 3-5 (Beta outreach → Early Access to existing users → Full Announcement)
**Key focus:** Existing user activation, upgrade/upsell opportunity, re-engagement of churned users
**Timeline:** 1-4 weeks
**Primary channels:** In-app, email to existing users, social media
**Extras:** Comparison page update, demo video, feature-specific landing page

### Scenario C: Incremental Update / Minor Feature
**Definition:** Improvements, bug fixes, UI polish, small additions that don't change the core value prop.
**Recommended path:** Skip phases, go straight to Ongoing Launch Strategy (Announcement Tactics)
**Key focus:** Signal active development, maintain engagement, show progress
**Timeline:** Same week as release
**Primary channels:** Changelog, social post, in-app notification, monthly roundup email

### Scenario D: Re-Launch / Pivot
**Definition:** Existing product with significantly repositioned messaging, target audience, or pricing.
**Recommended path:** Phase 2-5 (restart audience building, then re-launch to existing base)
**Key focus:** Resetting perception, reaching new audience segments, reactivating old leads with new angle
**Timeline:** 4-8 weeks
**Special:** Treat existing users as beta testers for the new positioning; get their buy-in first

## The ORB Framework

Structure your launch marketing across three channel types. Everything should ultimately lead back to owned channels.

### Channel Selection Matrix

Use this to decide where to focus first based on your situation:

| Situation | Start With | Secondary |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Pre-launch, no audience | Borrowed (borrow credibility fast) | Owned (capture borrowed attention) |
| Small existing list (<500) | Owned (deepen engagement) | Borrowed (accelerate growth) |
| Active social presence | Rented (amplify existing momentum) | Owned (convert social to owned) |
| B2B / enterprise | Borrowed (partner + guest content) | Owned (email + content marketing) |
| Consumer / viral potential | Rented (social hooks) | Borrowed (influencer seeding) |
| Limited time budget | Owned email only | Skip rented unless you have content ready |
| Limited money budget | Owned + Borrowed (organic only) | Avoid paid rented channels early |

**Decision rule:** Always have at least one owned channel active. Never rely on rented channels alone. Borrowed channels are fastest for cold starts.

### Owned Channels
You own the channel (though not the audience). Direct access without algorithms or platform rules.

**Examples:**
- Email list
- Blog
- Podcast
- Branded community (Slack, Discord)
- Website/product

**Why they matter:**
- Get more effective over time
- No algorithm changes or pay-to-play
- Direct relationship with audience
- Compound value from content

**Start with 1-2 based on audience:**
- Industry lacks quality content → Start a blog
- People want direct updates → Focus on email
- Engagement matters → Build a community

**Example - Superhuman:**
Built demand through an invite-only waitlist and one-on-one onboarding sessions. Every new user got a 30-minute live demo. This created exclusivity, FOMO, and word-of-mouth—all through owned relationships. Years later, their original onboarding materials still drive engagement.

### Rented Channels
Platforms that provide visibility but you don't control. Algorithms shift, rules change, pay-to-play increases.

**Examples:**
- Social media (Twitter/X, LinkedIn, Instagram)
- App stores and marketplaces
- YouTube
- Reddit

**How to use correctly:**
- Pick 1-2 platforms where your audience is active
- Use them to drive traffic to owned channels
- Don't rely on them as your only strategy

**Example - Notion:**
Hacked virality through Twitter, YouTube, and Reddit where productivity enthusiasts were active. Encouraged community to share templates and workflows. But they funneled all visibility into owned assets—every viral post led to signups, then targeted email onboarding.

**Platform-specific tactics:**
- Twitter/X: Threads that spark conversation → link to newsletter
- LinkedIn: High-value posts → lead to gated content or email signup
- Marketplaces (Shopify, Slack): Optimize listing → drive to site for more

Rented channels give speed, not stability. Capture momentum by bringing users into your owned ecosystem.

### Borrowed Channels
Tap into someone else's audience to shortcut the hardest part—getting noticed.

**Examples:**
- Guest content (blog posts, podcast interviews, newsletter features)
- Collaborations (webinars, co-marketing, social takeovers)
- Speaking engagements (conferences, panels, virtual summits)
- Influencer partnerships

**Be proactive, not passive:**
1. List industry leaders your audience follows
2. Pitch win-win collaborations
3. Use tools like SparkToro or Listen Notes to find audience overlap
4. Set up affiliate/referral incentives

**Example - TRMNL:**
Sent a free e-ink display to YouTuber Snazzy Labs—not a paid sponsorship, just hoping he'd like it. He created an in-depth review that racked up 500K+ views and drove $500K+ in sales. They also set up an affiliate program for ongoing promotion.

Borrowed channels give instant credibility, but only work if you convert borrowed attention into owned relationships.

## Waitlist Strategy

A waitlist isn't just a signup form. Done right, it's the core asset for a successful launch.

### Why Waitlists Work
- Creates perceived scarcity and FOMO
- Gives you a warm audience to launch to (they asked to hear from you)
- Provides an early signal of real demand vs. assumed demand
- Forces clear positioning — only people with the right problem sign up

### Waitlist Page Must-Haves
1. **Specific headline:** Name the problem you solve for a specific person. Not "the best X" — "the fastest way to do Y without Z."
2. **Social proof above the fold:** Even one early user quote beats generic copy
3. **Clear expectation:** When will they get access? What will they receive?
4. **Referral mechanic:** "Move up the waitlist by referring a friend" — turns each signup into a distribution node

### Waitlist Nurture (Before Launch Day)
Don't let your waitlist go cold. Email them:
- **Welcome email** (same day): Confirm signup, share your story briefly, set expectations
- **Behind the scenes** (weekly): Screenshots, building-in-public updates, early access previews
- **Problem content** (bi-weekly): Content about the problem you solve — builds credibility before you ask for anything
- **Tease features** (pre-launch): "Here's what's coming" builds excitement
- **The invitation** (launch day): Personalized, acknowledges the wait, makes it feel earned

### Waitlist Conversion Tactics
- **Batch access:** Invite 10-20% at a time to create urgency for those still waiting
- **Priority access for referrers:** Give referrers first access — your best-fit customers usually come from your earliest fans
- **"Jump the queue" trigger:** Early access for users who complete a survey, join a community, or watch a demo — filters for engaged users
- **Expiration:** Tell waitlisters their spot expires in 72 hours if they don't activate — drives activation from otherwise-cold signups

## Five-Phase Launch Approach

Launching isn't a one-day event. It's a phased process that builds momentum.

### Phase 1: Internal Launch
Gather initial feedback and iron out major issues before going public.

**Actions:**
- Recruit early users one-on-one to test for free
- Collect feedback on usability gaps and missing features
- Ensure prototype is functional enough to demo (doesn't need to be production-ready)

**Goal:** Validate core functionality with friendly users.

**When to advance:** At least 3-5 users have used the core flow end-to-end without hand-holding. No showstopper crashes or confusing UX blocks. You can articulate who gets the most value and why.

### Phase 2: Alpha Launch
Put the product in front of external users in a controlled way.

**Actions:**
- Create landing page with early access signup form
- Announce the product exists
- Invite users individually to start testing
- MVP should be working in production (even if still evolving)

**Goal:** First external validation and initial waitlist building.

**When to advance:** Landing page is live and converting. You have at least 20-50 people who've expressed interest. MVP runs in production (even rough). You've heard the same 2-3 pain points from multiple unrelated users.

### Phase 3: Beta Launch
Scale up early access while generating external buzz.

**Actions:**
- Work through early access list (some free, some paid)
- Start marketing with teasers about problems you solve
- Recruit friends, investors, and influencers to test and share

**Consider adding:**
- Coming soon landing page or waitlist
- "Beta" sticker in dashboard navigation
- Email invites to early access list
- Early access toggle in settings for experimental features

**Goal:** Build buzz and refine product with broader feedback.

**When to advance:** Core value prop is validated — users are getting meaningful outcomes. Waitlist has 100-500+ names. You know your top 3 use cases and can demo them cleanly. Retention in first week looks encouraging.

### Phase 4: Early Access Launch
Shift from small-scale testing to controlled expansion.

**Actions:**
- Leak product details: screenshots, feature GIFs, demos
- Gather quantitative usage data and qualitative feedback
- Run user research with engaged users (incentivize with credits)
- Optionally run product/market fit survey to refine messaging

**Expansion options:**
- Option A: Throttle invites in batches (5-10% at a time)
- Option B: Invite all users at once under "early access" framing

**Goal:** Validate at scale and prepare for full launch.

**When to advance:** Product/market fit signal is present — users are retaining and referring. You have enough usage data to write compelling launch copy. Support load is manageable. Payment flow works end-to-end. You have testimonials or case studies from beta users.

### Phase 5: Full Launch
Open the floodgates.

**Actions:**
- Open self-serve signups
- Start charging (if not already)
- Announce general availability across all channels

**Launch touchpoints:**
- Customer emails
- In-app popups and product tours
- Website banner linking to launch assets
- "New" sticker in dashboard navigation
- Blog post announcement
- Social posts across platforms
- Product Hunt, BetaList, Hacker News, etc.

**Goal:** Maximum visibility and conversion to paying users.

**When to advance:** N/A — this is the final public phase. Post-launch focus shifts to adoption, retention, and planning the next launch moment.

## Product Hunt Launch Strategy

Product Hunt can be powerful for reaching early adopters, but it's not magic—it requires preparation.

### Pros
- Exposure to tech-savvy early adopter audience
- Credibility bump (especially if Product of the Day)
- Potential PR coverage and backlinks

### Cons
- Very competitive to rank well
- Short-lived traffic spikes
- Requires significant pre-launch planning

### When NOT to Launch on Product Hunt
- You have no existing audience to mobilize on launch day (you need votes to climb the rankings)
- Your product isn't relevant to tech-savvy early adopters (B2B enterprise, local businesses, etc.)
- You're not prepared to engage all day — Product Hunt requires active, real-time participation
- You're launching something that already has massive traction elsewhere (Product Hunt adds noise, not signal)

### Timing Strategy
- **Best days:** Tuesday or Wednesday (highest traffic, less competition than Monday)
- **Best time to post:** 12:01 AM PST (gives maximum 24-hour window)
- **Avoid:** Holidays, Conference weeks (WWDC, SaaStr, etc.), days when a major competitor is also launching
- **Research first:** Browse recent launches in your category to understand competition density and what visuals/copy are working

### How to Launch Successfully

**Before launch day:**
1. Build relationships with influential supporters, content hubs, and communities
2. Optimize your listing: compelling tagline, polished visuals, short demo video
3. Study successful launches to identify what worked
4. Engage in relevant communities—provide value before pitching
5. Prepare your team for all-day engagement

**On launch day:**
1. Treat it as an all-day event
2. Respond to every comment in real-time
3. Answer questions and spark discussions
4. Encourage your existing audience to engage
5. Direct traffic back to your site to capture signups

**After launch day:**
1. Follow up with everyone who engaged
2. Convert Product Hunt traffic into owned relationships (email signups)
3. Continue momentum with post-launch content

### Case Studies

**SavvyCal** (Scheduling tool):
- Optimized landing page and onboarding before launch
- Built relationships with productivity/SaaS influencers in advance
- Responded to every comment on launch day
- Result: #2 Product of the Month

**Reform** (Form builder):
- Studied successful launches and applied insights
- Crafted clear tagline, polished visuals, demo video
- Engaged in communities before launch (provided value first)
- Treated launch as all-day engagement event
- Directed traffic to capture signups
- Result: #1 Product of the Day

## Post-Launch Product Marketing

Your launch isn't over when the announcement goes live. Now comes adoption and retention work.

### Immediate Post-Launch Actions

**Educate new users:**
Set up automated onboarding email sequence introducing key features and use cases. The sequence should cover:
- Email 1 (Day 0): Welcome + first meaningful action to take
- Email 2 (Day 2): Show them one specific success path (the job-to-be-done they came for)
- Email 3 (Day 5): Address the top objection or confusion you've heard in support
- Email 4 (Day 10): Social proof + advanced use case they haven't explored yet
- Email 5 (Day 21): "Are you getting value?" check-in + clear upgrade/referral CTA

**Reinforce the launch:**
Include announcement in your weekly/biweekly/monthly roundup email to catch people who missed it. Re-frame the announcement with new social proof or data if available.

**Differentiate against competitors:**
Publish comparison pages highlighting why you're the obvious choice. (See **programmatic-seo** skill for structure.)

**Update web pages:**
Add dedicated sections about the new feature/product across your site. Update the hero if it was a major launch.

**Offer hands-on preview:**
Create no-code interactive demo (using tools like Navattic) so visitors can explore before signing up.

**Reactivate cold leads:**
Email your list of people who signed up but never activated. Use launch as the reason to re-engage: "We've just shipped X — now might be the right time to give us another look."

**Capture and publish social proof:**
Within 2 weeks of launch, proactively ask your most active users for testimonials, case studies, or quick quotes. Launch momentum creates the best window for collecting social proof.

### Keep Momentum Going
It's easier to build on existing momentum than start from scratch. Every touchpoint reinforces the launch.

## Ongoing Launch Strategy

Don't rely on a single launch event. Regular updates and feature rollouts sustain engagement.

### How to Prioritize What to Announce

Use this matrix to decide how much marketing each update deserves:

**Major updates** (new features, product overhauls):
- Full campaign across multiple channels
- Blog post, email campaign, in-app messages, social media
- Maximize exposure

**Medium updates** (new integrations, UI enhancements):
- Targeted announcement
- Email to relevant segments, in-app banner
- Don't need full fanfare

**Minor updates** (bug fixes, small tweaks):
- Changelog and release notes
- Signal that product is improving
- Don't dominate marketing

### Announcement Tactics

**Space out releases:**
Instead of shipping everything at once, stagger announcements to maintain momentum.

**Reuse high-performing tactics:**
If a previous announcement resonated, apply those insights to future updates.

**Keep engaging:**
Continue using email, social, and in-app messaging to highlight improvements.

**Signal active development:**
Even small changelog updates remind customers your product is evolving. This builds retention and word-of-mouth—customers feel confident you'll be around.

## Launch Momentum Metrics

Track these signals to know if your launch is working. Don't just ship and hope.

### Pre-Launch Health Signals
| Metric | Weak | Strong |
|--------|------|--------|
| Waitlist signup rate | <5% landing page CVR | >15% CVR |
| Email open rate (waitlist nurture) | <25% | >45% |
| Inbound requests / DMs | Mostly silent | 5-10+ unsolicited outreach/week |
| Borrowed channel acceptance rate | <10% of outreach responds | >30% responds positively |

### Launch Day Signals (First 48h)
| Metric | Weak | Strong |
|--------|------|--------|
| Email click-through rate | <3% | >8% |
| Signup conversion from announcement | <2% of email list signs up | >10% |
| Social engagement | <50 total engagements | >500 engagements |
| Support / question volume | Near zero (disinterest) | Busy (good sign — people care) |

### Post-Launch Health (Week 1-4)
| Metric | Weak | Strong |
|--------|------|--------|
| Activation rate (users who hit core value) | <20% | >50% |
| D7 retention | <20% | >40% |
| Referral rate (organic signups from referrals) | <5% of new users | >20% |
| Expansion revenue / upgrades | None | Early upgrade signals present |

**Key insight:** If pre-launch signals are weak, don't delay and hope launch day fixes it. Revisit your messaging, your audience targeting, or your borrowed channel mix first.

## Common Launch Mistakes (Anti-Patterns)

These patterns kill launches. Avoid them.

### 1. Waiting Until Perfect
**Pattern:** Delaying launch to add more features, fix minor bugs, or polish edge cases.
**Why it fails:** You lose real user feedback that would have changed what you build. The product never feels "ready" because there's always more to add.
**Fix:** Launch the moment the core value prop works. Ship ugly if needed. Iterate in public.

### 2. Treating Launch Day as the Finish Line
**Pattern:** Treating the announcement as the end of launch work.
**Why it fails:** Most potential users won't see the day-1 post. Launch is a campaign, not a moment.
**Fix:** Plan 2-4 weeks of post-launch content, follow-ups, and announcements.

### 3. Skipping the Owned Channel Foundation
**Pattern:** Going all-in on Product Hunt, Twitter, or Reddit with no owned channel to capture interest.
**Why it fails:** Platform traffic spikes and dies. You have no way to re-engage people who showed interest.
**Fix:** Always have an email capture before launch. The landing page goal is email collection first, signups second.

### 4. Launching to the Wrong Audience First
**Pattern:** Going broad on launch day instead of seeding with your highest-signal early adopters.
**Why it fails:** Generic audiences give noisy feedback. Low activation rates make your metrics look bad.
**Fix:** Start with people who specifically have the problem you solve. Expand from there.

### 5. Neglecting Borrowed Channel Outreach Pre-Launch
**Pattern:** Waiting until launch week to reach out to influencers, podcasters, and community leaders.
**Why it fails:** They need weeks of lead time. A last-minute pitch usually gets ignored.
**Fix:** Start borrowed channel outreach 4-6 weeks before launch. Build the relationship first.

### 6. Generic Messaging / Category Framing
**Pattern:** Describing yourself as "a project management tool" or "an email marketing platform" — the category, not the point of difference.
**Why it fails:** No one shares generic category copy. It doesn't land for the people who'd actually care.
**Fix:** Lead with the specific outcome, the specific user, or the specific problem. "For founders who hate spreadsheets, not for enterprise teams with IT departments."

### 7. Ignoring Timing and Context
**Pattern:** Launching on a Monday before a major holiday, or the same day as a competitor or industry event that will dominate attention.
**Why it fails:** Launch success depends heavily on whether the right people are paying attention.
**Fix:** Research your audience's calendar. The best launch windows are Tuesday-Thursday when engagement is highest and there's no competing event.

- [ ] Landing page with clear value proposition

- [ ] Announcement email to list
- [ ] Blog post published
- [ ] Social posts scheduled and posted

## Task-Specific Questions

**Scenario identification (ask first):**
1. What are you launching? → determines which Scenario (A/B/C/D) applies and which phases to use
2. Where are you in the process? (Just starting → Full phases. Already have users → Phase 4-5)
3. What's your timeline? (< 2 weeks → skip phases, compress to essentials)

**Channel and audience:**
4. What owned channels do you have? (Email list: how many? Blog: monthly traffic? Community: active?)
5. What rented channels are you active on? Where does your audience spend time?
6. Any borrowed channel relationships? (Influencers, podcast hosts, community admins who know you)

**Context:**
7. Have you launched before? What happened? What worked, what didn't?
8. Do you have budget for paid promotion? Or is this organic-only?
9. Are you considering Product Hunt? If yes, how much prep time do you have?

**Output format:**
After gathering context, deliver a launch plan with:
1. **Scenario classification** (A/B/C/D) with recommended phase path
2. **Channel recommendation** using the selection matrix (which channels, in what priority order)
3. **Timeline** with phase milestones and "When to advance" gates
4. **Top 3 priorities** for this specific launch (not generic advice — specific to their situation)
5. **Anti-patterns to watch for** based on their context
6. **Launch checklist** tailored to their phase and channels (don't output the full generic checklist — filter to what applies)
